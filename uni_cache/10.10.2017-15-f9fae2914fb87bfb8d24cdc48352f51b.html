Where could students impact the 2017 general election?

\n
\n\n \n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n

Times Higher Education data analysis pinpoints the universities whose students could hold some sway

\n
\n\n \n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n May 18 2017
\n\n \n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n
\n\n \n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n
  • \n \n By Simon Baker\nEmail simon.baker@timeshighereducation.com
    • \n
\n \n \n \n \n \n \n
\n\n \n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n

The UK\xe2\x80\x99s snap general election has given universities and student organisations a very short amount of time to get their message across to students ensuring they are registered to vote.

\n

However, a new data analysis by Times Higher Education reveals just how important those voters could be, especially in crucial battleground seats where the Conservatives are looking to strengthen their parliamentary majority by taking Labour-held constituencies.

\n

\n\n \n \n \n
\n

\n

\n
\n\n \n \n
\n

\n

Marrying census statistics on the number of students in constituencies with data on each university\xe2\x80\x99s own student population (see here for full methodology), the analysis pinpoints exactly which universities could have the biggest influence on results in terms of the votes of their students.

\n

Universities that score highly (see table below) \xe2\x80\x93 owing to their campuses having a large share of students in nearby constituencies that had close results in the 2015 general election \xe2\x80\x93 include the University of Cambridge (where Labour narrowly beat the Liberal Democrats last time), Lancaster University (close to the ultra-marginal Labour seat of Lancaster and Fleetwood) and the University of Edinburgh (close to seats where the Scottish National Party, Labour and Liberal Democrats all have a reasonable chance of winning).

\n

\n

For students to sway a constituency result, a number of conditions need to be met, not least that enough vote at their term-time address. The general election will be held at a time when many students will have finished exams and be drifting away from campus.\xc2\xa0A poll commissioned by the Higher Education Policy Institute earlier this month showed that only 25 per cent of students planned to vote in their \xe2\x80\x9cuniversity constituency\xe2\x80\x9d rather than their \xe2\x80\x9chome address\xe2\x80\x9d.

\n

However, the National Union of Students\xc2\xa0campaign to get students registered by the 22 May deadline has been reminding them that they can be registered at both a term-time and home address, and choose to vote in one of the two.

\n

Rob Young, NUS vice-president for society and citizenship, said it was important that students were aware of all their options, which could also include voting by post from home but for their university seat.

\n

He added that the NUS would also \xe2\x80\x9csupport [local] student unions and let them know about the constituencies where the margins are quite small\xe2\x80\x9d.

\n

Nick Hillman, director of Hepi and a former Conservative general election candidate, said it would be \xe2\x80\x9ctotally legitimate\xe2\x80\x9d if students choose to vote in their term-time constituency, or likewise their home seat, due there being a higher probability they could impact the result.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cYou are just playing by the rules of a first past the post electoral system. It is a bit like tactical voting. Nobody would ever think it appropriate to ban tactical voting,\xe2\x80\x9d he said.\xc2\xa0

\n

But he warned that the likelihood of students doing this assumed they had a keen enough interest in politics to know how marginal constituencies actually were.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cYou have to be as obsessed with politics as I am to think about your vote at that depth,\xe2\x80\x9d said Mr Hillman. \xe2\x80\x9cThe best way to do it is to organise a postal vote in one of the two seats but that takes a lot of organisation that people can\xe2\x80\x99t always be bothered with,\xe2\x80\x9d he added.

\n

Work in UK higher education? Take our two-minute general election survey

\n

THE\xe2\x80\x99s analysis \xe2\x80\x93 devised by World University Rankings data scientist Emma Deraze \xe2\x80\x93 could help give students such information. It includes satellite campuses to show that some institutions\xe2\x80\x99 students might have a say across a wide geographical area.

\n

A key example is the top-scoring institution \xe2\x80\x93 the University of Derby \xe2\x80\x93 which has students spread across three campuses, two of them outside Derby. The city itself is split between Derby North, a very narrow Tory win in 2015, and Labour-held Derby South, which the Tories are sure to target this time. \xc2\xa0

\n

According to Stephen Fisher, associate professor in political sociology at the University of Oxford and co-author of a Hepi report before the last election on student voting patterns, the seats where students may have the most impact would most likely be those further down the Tory target list where students could \xe2\x80\x9csave them\xe2\x80\x9d for Labour.

\n

This would assume that Hepi\xe2\x80\x99s poll this month showing 55 per cent student support for Labour does translate into more votes for the party in student areas.

\n

But Professor Fisher said, given this, \xe2\x80\x9cseats that are in the balance and where students might be most effective" included places like Exeter (the University of Exeter comes 19th on THE\xe2\x80\x99s list), Nottingham South (both Nottingham universities are in the top half of the list), York Central and Norwich South. Derby South, mentioned above, might also fit into this category.\xc2\xa0Professor Fisher also highlighted the potential importance of students in Edinburgh\xe2\x80\x99s constituencies.\xc2\xa0

\n

He added that his previous research had shown that in student areas there can be a higher, but small, swing towards a particular party than the national average, although in reality this rarely changed the result. For instance, anger over the Lib Dems breaking their tuition fees pledge may have led to a fall in the party\xe2\x80\x99s vote of two percentage points above the average in student areas and their losing the Cambridge constituency to Labour.

\n

A two-point extra slant towards Labour this time in some student areas on the Tory target list \xe2\x80\x9cmight be enough" to help the party \xe2\x80\x9chold on to a handful\xe2\x80\x9d of seats, he said.

\n

Mr Hillman added that academics\xe2\x80\x99 voting intentions should also be thrown into this mix. \xe2\x80\x9cUniversity constituencies include a lot of university staff,\xe2\x80\x9d he pointed out, \xe2\x80\x9c...and we know, not least from polls [carried out by THE], that academics tend to vote more left-wing than the rest of the country\xe2\x80\x9d.

\n

As for the issues that may be important for students on 8 June, the obvious candidate would be tuition fees and Labour\xe2\x80\x99s pledge to scrap them. But Mr Hillman warned that students were often just as likely to be interested in issues affecting the wider population, like Brexit.

\n

Mr Young also made a similar point, saying that in his view Brexit, and the damage he said it would potentially do to students\xe2\x80\x99 futures, was higher up on their list of concerns.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cI think students do want to see a fairer funding system [for higher education]...but I think when students go to the ballot box [Brexit] is the thing that they will think most about.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n
