Salary increases for Russell Group v-cs beat staff pay rises again

\n
\n\n \n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n

Average cost of remunerating v-cs in group rose by 6 per cent last year but salaries still lag those in top US and Australian universities

Up, up and away: the UCU\xe2\x80\x99s Sally Hunt suggested that \xe2\x80\x98calls for self-restraint in v-c remuneration are falling on deaf ears\xe2\x80\x99

\n \n \n \n
\n

The cost of paying the leaders of the UK\xe2\x80\x99s top universities has soared yet again despite government warnings over rising executive pay, new figures show.

\n

While rank-and-file academics received a 1 per cent pay rise in 2015-16, the average cost of paying the salary and benefits of a Russell Group vice-chancellor increased by 5.9 per cent to \xc2\xa3342,200 in the same year \xe2\x80\x93 about \xc2\xa319,000 more than in 2014-15, according to a Times Higher Education analysis of 21 of the group\xe2\x80\x99s 24 members.

\n

Once pensions are included, the average cost of pay packages stood at \xc2\xa3366,500 \xe2\x80\x93 5.2 per cent higher than the average cost of the equivalent remuneration in 2014-15.

\n

The news is likely to cause further dismay among university staff who staged a two-day walkout last May over this academic year\xe2\x80\x99s 1.1 per cent pay offer. Staff also saw take-home pay fall last April as employee pension contributions increased, costing thousands of staff about \xc2\xa3100 a month.

\n

This year\xe2\x80\x99s executive pay figures, released in newly published university accounts, also reveal how several Russell Group vice-chancellors received hefty bonuses, while one was given more than \xc2\xa3250,000 on retiring a year earlier than planned.

\n

According to the University of Southampton\xe2\x80\x99s latest financial statements, Don Nutbeam, its former vice-chancellor,\xc2\xa0was paid \xc2\xa3252,000 as \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation for loss of office\xe2\x80\x9d in addition to a \xc2\xa343,000 \xe2\x80\x9cperformance-related bonus\xe2\x80\x9d before stepping down at the end of September 2015, bringing his total remuneration for 2015-16 to \xc2\xa3345,000.

\n

With \xc2\xa3352,000, including pensions contributions, paid to the new vice-chancellor Sir Christopher Snowden for the remaining 10 months of 2015-16, the cost of remuneration for Southampton\xe2\x80\x99s head totalled \xc2\xa3697,000 that year.

\n

Gill Rider, the university\xe2\x80\x99s chair of council, said that the opportunity to recruit Sir Christopher had arisen \xe2\x80\x9cmore quickly than planned or anticipated\xe2\x80\x9d after Professor Nutbeam announced in July 2014 that he wished to retire at the end of his contract in September 2016 or before, if a suitable replacement was found.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cProfessor Nutbeam was then able to leave and the university paid him until the end of his contract,\xe2\x80\x9d said Dr Rider, who added that the new vice-chancellor\xe2\x80\x99s annual salary of \xc2\xa3419,000 reflected the former Universities UK president\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cproven track record\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cquality and high level of expertise which he brings to the university\xe2\x80\x9d.

\n

However, such pay-offs and higher salaries should invite closer public scrutiny as government ministers warned about overly generous severance payments in the March 2016 letter on university grants, said Sally Hunt, general secretary of the University and College Union.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIt\xe2\x80\x99s clear that the minister\xe2\x80\x99s calls for self-restraint in vice-chancellor remuneration are falling on deaf ears and we need more transparency in how these bumper pay deals are agreed,\xe2\x80\x9d said Ms Hunt.

\n

Professor Nutbeam\xe2\x80\x99s final pay package, and transition arrangements at other universities, contributed heavily to rising leadership costs, our analysis shows. Once they are removed from calculations, Russell Group vice-chancellors\xe2\x80\x99 average salaries rose by a more modest 3.7 per cent last year, or 3.3 per cent, if pensions are included.

\n

A total of seven Russell Group universities paid their leaders more than \xc2\xa3400,000 last year.

\n

Source:\xc2\xa0
University accounts
Notes: *Left the institution in 2015-16. Does not include King\xe2\x80\x99s College London, the London School of Economics and University College London, whose accounts were not available at the time of compilation. Some figures have been rounded
\n

Considering the institutions with one leader in 2015-16, Alice Gast, president of Imperial College London, topped the UK pay league, with a total pay package of \xc2\xa3430,000 once an \xc2\xa318,000 accommodation allowance and \xc2\xa359,000 in pension contributions are included.

\n

Sir David Eastwood, vice-chancellor of the University of Birmingham, received a total package of \xc2\xa3426,000 \xe2\x80\x93 \xc2\xa310,000 (2.4 per cent) more than in 2014-15 \xe2\x80\x93 while \xc2\xa3426,000 was also awarded to Sir Steve Smith, vice-chancellor of the University of Exeter, whose total pay climbed by \xc2\xa326,000 (6.5 per cent) last year.

\n

The University of Oxford paid a total of \xc2\xa3442,000 to the two vice-chancellors it had in 2015-16, with Louise Richardson awarded \xc2\xa3204,000 in salary and \xc2\xa335,000 in pension contributions for the first seven months of 2016.

\n

Other high earners include Sir Keith Burnett, vice-chancellor of the University of Sheffield, whose \xc2\xa3385,000 salary was enhanced last year by a bonus worth \xc2\xa333,296 \xe2\x80\x93 pushing his total\xc2\xa0pay\xc2\xa0to \xc2\xa3422,706 in 2015-16, 9.7 per cent higher than in 2014-15.

\n

Sir Keith\xe2\x80\x99s remuneration reflected his role as the head of a \xe2\x80\x9clarge and complex organisation with an annual turnover of about \xc2\xa3600 million\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cimportance the university attaches to having a leader in higher education, who is highly respected within and beyond the sector, both in the UK and internationally\xe2\x80\x9d, said Tony Pedder, the university\xe2\x80\x99s chair of council.

\n

Wendy Piatt, director general of the Russell Group, pointed out that \xe2\x80\x9cmany vice-chancellors have accepted only very modest increases\xe2\x80\x9d, but that pay levels were set by independent remuneration committees that included \xe2\x80\x9cexpert representatives from outside the sector\xe2\x80\x9d.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThese experts understand the importance of attracting and retaining experienced individuals who can demonstrate the highest calibre of leadership and determine the appropriate level of award,\xe2\x80\x9d Dr Piatt said.

\n

Despite the rising pay of UK vice-chancellors, average pay still lagged behind the amounts awarded to the leaders of elite universities in the US and Australia, analysis by THE shows.

\n

According to data published by\xc2\xa0The Chronicle of Higher Education last month, in 2014-15 \xe2\x80\x93 the latest available figures \xe2\x80\x93 the average total compensation paid to an Ivy League president was $1,441,451 (or \xc2\xa3923,753 when the conversion was made in 2014) \xe2\x80\x93 7.2 per cent lower than the previous year. \xc2\xa0

\n

Amy Gutmann, president of the University of Pennsylvania, was the Ivy League\xe2\x80\x99s highest earner on $2,962,708 (\xc2\xa31,903,000 in 2014), with five leaders in the eight-strong group earning less than $1 million (\xc2\xa3640,850 in 2014) in 2014-15.

\n

Source:\xc2\xa0
The Chronicle of Higher Education
Notes: Sterling conversions taken at the end of the 2014 financial year. Figures have been rounded. *Has since left the institution
\n

In Australia\xe2\x80\x99s Group of Eight university group, average salaries in 2015, not including pension contributions, stood at A$1,091,000 (\xc2\xa3536,320 when the conversion was made in 2015), 4 per cent higher than in 2014, according to information collated by The Australian. Michael Spence, vice-chancellor of the University of Sydney, was the highest earner on A$1,385,000 (\xc2\xa3680,846 in 2015), the paper reported.

\n

Source:\xc2\xa0
The Australian
Notes: Figures for 12 months up to 31 December 2015. Sterling conversions taken at the end of 2015. Some figures have been rounded
