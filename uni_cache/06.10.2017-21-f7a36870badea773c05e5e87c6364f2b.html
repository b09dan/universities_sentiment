Chinese scholars \xe2\x80\x98win up to \xc2\xa3127,000\xe2\x80\x99 for papers in top journals

\n
\n\n \n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n

Analysis reveals extent of reward system for international publications, which could be distorting scientific incentives

\n\n \n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n July 14, 2017

Chinese scientists can win awards of up to $165,000 (\xc2\xa3127,000) from their universities for publishing in leading scientific journals, according to research that exposes the country\xe2\x80\x99s system of \xe2\x80\x9ccash for articles\xe2\x80\x9d.

\n

Academics in the West have long feared that competition to be published in the most prestigious, high-profile journals in order to achieve a career boost may be distorting scientific incentives.

\n

But a new paper details the extent to which Chinese universities are going one step further and openly offering large sums in return for publication.

\n

Science and Nature are the most lucrative journals, netting Chinese scientists an average of $43,783 per publication (one university policy sets out a reward of $165,000), an increase of two-thirds since 2008.

\n

The average professorial salary in China is $8,600.

\n

To the surprise of the paper\xe2\x80\x99s authors, \xe2\x80\x9cTier 3\xe2\x80\x9d universities \xe2\x80\x93 that is, those institutions that are not part of special research funding programmes \xe2\x80\x93 offer bigger rewards to scientists than their richer counterparts. A scientist at a Tier 3 university who published in Science or Nature bags an average of $63,187, they found.

\n

The authors scoured the internet to find the policies of 100 Chinese universities, drawn from across the country and at different levels of research prestige.

\n

At some universities, scientists are given cash rewards depending on the journal impact factor (JIF) of the outlet in which they publish. Others pay out based on how many citations a paper racks up in a certain time frame.

\n

Since 2005, the authors found, Chinese universities have increasingly shifted to handing out rewards based on which quartile a publication falls in Clarivate Analytics\xe2\x80\x99 Journal Citation Reports.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe average amount of cash award has increased over the past 10 years, except that the amount awarded to papers published in journals with low JIF has decreased,\xe2\x80\x9d the authors write.

\n

Universities focus on rewarding publication in journals included in Clarivate\xe2\x80\x99s Web of Science (WoS) database, which helps institutions to climb international rankings and receive funding. The authors conclude that the system of cash for papers has led to a \xe2\x80\x9cradical increase in China\xe2\x80\x99s international scholarly publication\xe2\x80\x9d.

\n

But ignoring journals outside the Web of Science\xc2\xa0means that many papers published in Chinese journals are \xe2\x80\x9calmost ignored and excluded from the cash reward\xe2\x80\x9d.

\n

And using bibliometric indicators such as the JIF to determine payouts is \xe2\x80\x9cabusive\xe2\x80\x9d, the authors conclude, as it is \xe2\x80\x9cwidely recognised to be a poor metric for evaluating the quality of individual papers\xe2\x80\x9d.

\n

The rewards can also encourage scientists to spam journals with vast numbers of articles in order to maximise their return, the authors warn. They cite the example of one professor who between 2004 and 2009 published 279 papers in one journal, winning more than half the rewards given out by his university during the period.

\n

His \xe2\x80\x9conly research focus in these five years was to find new crystal structures in his lab and always report the results of this to the same journal, because he could accomplish the goal of winning the cash bonus in [the] short term as contrasted with receiving fewer awards by conducting long-term research projects\xe2\x80\x9d, the authors say.

\n

The article, \xe2\x80\x9cPublish or impoverish: An investigation of the monetary reward system of science in China (1999-2016)\xe2\x80\x9d, is currently on the arXiv preprints repository.

\n

Its authors are Wei Quan, of the School of Information Management at Wuhan University; Bikun Chen, from the School of Economics and Management at Nanjing University of Science and Technology; and Fei Shu, of the School of Information Studies at McGill University.
